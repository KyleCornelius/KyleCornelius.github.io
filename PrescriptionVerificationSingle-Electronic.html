Prescription Verification Single - Electronic

 

Introduction

PVS-E (Prescription Verification Single - Electronic) is a pharmacist application used to verify and fill a single prescription. The most complex medications and highest-risk customers are routed through PVS. Therefore, it was important to be designed in a way that minimizes patient risk, but is also as efficient as possible for our in-house pharmacists.

Goals

  1. Re-imagine the verification process. Modernize the process to be more in line with current technology and other leading pharmacy clearinghouses. Automate as much of the process as possible; let the system work for the user.
  2. Increase rates at which pharmacists can verify a prescription. Improve the layout to make information easy to consume by reducing clutter/ noise and focusing on the information needed at the time of verification.
  3. Improve trust with the system. Employ modern iconography, typography, and visual cues to alert users and covey meanings quickly. Give reassurance to the user that the data is true and the system is taking care of particular tasks for the user.

Impact Summary

  • 40% increase in efficiency for prescription verification rates
  • Migrated 229 HDP users and 295 SP users
  • Rx Field errors reduced by 67%
  • $3.5M annual labor cost savings

Pilot 1.0

This was the first modern development for this department, and first time utilizing a UX team, so overall maturity was low. Because of this, silos existed, collaboration and user access was limited, and the overall organizational structure was unorganized. Verification rates were 35% worse in the pilot compared to the legacy system, IRIS.

Pilot Issues

Initial development of 1.0 included very limited user research and usability testing. Pilot users had issues with:

  • Too much scrolling
  • Difficulty locating appropriate data
  • Inconsistent flows and patterns resulting in user behavior that has potential to adversely affect patient safety

Pilot Lessons Learned

The UX team failed to be a strong voice and extrapolate value to stakeholders. Moving forward the team:

  • Educated stakeholders on the UX discipline and its value
  • Shared all research and testing results, including insights and how those insights were analyzed to influence decisions
  • Scheduled regular touch points with subject matter experts and stakeholders

Research for 2.0

Several user interviews, observation sessions, and SME panel sessions were conducted to better understand our users and how to move forward in 2.0

Key Takeaways:

  • Our users are highly trained and will be working in this application to do the majority of their work every day. Designs should be practical and functional over intuitive.
  • Patient health and the pharmacist’s license are "on the line" if they make mistakes. Because of this, if data it is on the screen users will read it.
  • Pharmacists always check the patient and prescription information, most of the other information is ancillary and may or may not be needed for a particular patient.

Usability Testing

Multiple prototypes were tested to understand how users interact with them. The overarching goal was to find the strenghts and weaknesses of each prototype and combine that data to iterate upon.

Goals

  • Identify user experience problems within the prototypes
    • Navigation errors - excessive mousing and/or scrolling, failure to follow intended screen flow, difficulty finding functionalities
    • Presentation errors - difficulty finding pieces of data, selection errors, ambiguity in labeling of implied design
    • Control usage errors - improper use of fields, functionalities, etc.
  • Learn positive and negative common patterns and behaviors across users that are tested
  • Collect data to determine if proof of concept is effective, efficient, and well-received

Analysis

Example of the movement pattern on one of the prototypes.

Findings

  • System feedback is important; indicators that tell users the system has verified certain pieces of data helps users spend less time verifiying and they are starting to trust data
  • Users mostly prefer rx Hx to be closer to the rx form
  • Presentation errors - difficulty finding pieces of data, selection errors, ambiguity in labeling of implied design
  • The top rx summary bar serves little to no purpose for users
  • Users need more information to identify a patient if not verified by system (three identifiers)
  • Accessing/identifying comms and verses needs to be easier and more visible or clear
  • DE reminders and DAW2 Preferences need more prominence as they are a big source for errors

Moving Forward

  • Place emphasis on the kinds of system feedback that users responded well to
  • Swap positions of rx Hx and Notes form
  • Remove top summary bar
  • Make patient identification easier
  • Comms needs more prominence, easier acessibility, and clarity
  • DE reminders and DAW2 Preferences need to be more prominent as they are a big source for errors
  • Work through brainstorm items that the UX team needs more clarification on that came out of the usability sessions

Usability Testing - Further Iteration

Findings

  • There is still work to be done on flagging on errors vs. system verified data
  • Users are more pleased with this layout and felt that its design helped their PV process
  • Many of the recommended changes to finalize the prototypes from this point moving forward are minor
  • Users are easily able to identify the data they were looking for
  • It takes a while for users to match up rx data fields
  • Hot keys will greatly improve upon this experience

Moving Forward

  • More emphasis on warning flags for mismatches
  • Icons for ES SIG, Copy SIG, and reset need to be added
  • Auto AIT need to be added with the following acknowledge modal upon approval before proceeding to the next rx in the queue
  • When there is a GPI Match, more emphasis needs to be place in the rx HX in order for users to recognize medication must be discontinued
  • rx data fields may need to be shuffled to compare more easily
  • Ability to access more prescriber information

Project Lessons Learned

  • UX needs to have a strong voice and bring the stakeholders along for the journey. Always advocate for best practices.
  • Clear system feedback built tremendous trust between the system and user over time.
  • Less is more. Only supplying the pharmacists with the information they always require on the primary view significantly increases efficiency. housing other data in secondary views allowed to ease the prescribers.
  • Pilot 1.0 highlights how important user research is. This cannot be stated enough. Research is required before jumping straight into wireframes.
